# What Is a Cyberdeck?

A cyberdeck is a custom-built portable computer — part art project, part functional tool, part statement piece. The term comes from William Gibson's 1984 novel *Neuromancer*, where characters used handheld devices to jack into cyberspace. Today's cyberdecks are tangible machines built by makers who want something more personal than a mass-produced laptop.

In early 2026, cyberdecks broke out of the niche maker world and into mainstream culture when women and femme builders on TikTok started turning purses, Polly Pocket compacts, Pokémon tins, and fast-food boxes into working computers — and millions of people watched. The cyberdeck went from a gray-box hobby to a craft movement practically overnight.

## What Makes a Cyberdeck?

There's no single definition, but most cyberdecks share a few traits:

- **Custom enclosure** — This is where it gets fun. Pelican cases were the original go-to, but the 2026 wave has blown the doors wide open: clamshell purses, 3D-printed shells, vintage lunchboxes, makeup caboodles, Lego bricks, seashell clutches, dinosaur toys, and even a [Dunkin' Donuts box](https://www.tiktok.com/@cocoasaesthetic). If it can hold a tiny computer *(a single-board computer like a Raspberry Pi)*, it can be a cyberdeck.
- **A tiny computer** — A Raspberry Pi is the most popular, but plenty of builders use LattePanda, Radxa, or Framework boards. The Pi is affordable (around $35–75 depending on the model) and has a huge community behind it, which makes troubleshooting a lot less lonely.
- **Integrated display** — From tiny 3.5-inch screens to e-ink panels to ultrawide monitors. Some builders go screenless and use their deck as a portable server instead.
- **Keyboard** — Mechanical keyboards, split ergonomic layouts, repurposed BlackBerry keyboards for ultra-compact builds, or off-the-shelf mini keyboards from M5Stack or similar.
- **Portable power** — Battery packs, USB-C PD *(Power Delivery)* banks, or custom lithium-polymer setups.
- **Personality** — This is the part that matters most. Every deck reflects who built it.

## Why Build a Cyberdeck?

People build cyberdecks for all kinds of reasons:

- **Learning electronics** — Soldering, wiring, power management, and Linux administration. Many builders say their cyberdeck was the first time they ever soldered anything.
- **Creative expression** — Designing something that looks and feels uniquely yours. For a lot of the TikTok-era builders, this is the primary motivation — the deck is the medium.
- **Practical use** — Field computing, security testing, ham radio, off-grid work, writing, journaling, or loading up offline copies of Wikipedia, maps, music, and books.
- **Reclaiming your tech** — A growing number of builders frame their decks as a deliberate step away from sealed-shut, surveilled consumer electronics and AI-saturated platforms. More on that below.
- **Community** — Joining a global movement of builders who share designs, help each other troubleshoot, and celebrate each other's work.

You don't need to be an expert to start. Many first-time builders create beautiful, functional decks with off-the-shelf parts and no soldering at all. [Alexine's beginner build guide](https://jalexine.github.io/lab/build-your-first-cyberdeck.html) and the [Cyberdeck Cafe build guide](https://cyberdeck.cafe/build) are two of the friendliest starting points.

## The 2026 TikTok Wave

In March 2026, a London-based creator named Annike Tan ([@ubeboobey on TikTok](https://www.tiktok.com/@ubeboobey)) posted a video of a pearlescent clamshell-purse cyberdeck — a "mermaid cyberdeck" with seashell headphones, a gold-chain mouse, and a Raspberry Pi running Doom, offline music, and an entire copy of Wikipedia. The video went everywhere. Within weeks, her cyberdeck content had been viewed over [32 million times](https://www.wired.com/story/cyberdecks-tiktok-ube-boobey-diy-computer-gadget/), with roughly 75% of her audience being women — a near-total inversion of the cyberdeck community's previous demographics.

What followed was a creative explosion. Builders across TikTok started making their own decks in enclosures that had nothing to do with the old Pelican-case orthodoxy:

### Why It Caught On

The femme cyberdeck wave isn't about a different technology — the Raspberry Pi, the screens, the keyboards, and the soldering techniques are the same ones builders have been using for years. What changed is **who is building, why, and inside what**.

A lot of the new builders describe their decks as a response to how modern consumer tech feels: sealed shut, surveilled, designed by someone else for someone else. As Tan put it in [an interview](https://yapyear.com/p/why-is-everyone-talking-about-cyberdecks), the goal is to keep personal devices away from AI and megacorp ecosystems. The widely-shared rallying cry in her comment sections — "not doomsday, solarpunk" — captures how the 2026 wave has reframed the cyberdeck from survivalist console to **whimsical personal artifact**.

The aesthetic vocabulary is wide: barbiecore, cottagecore, Y2K nostalgia, kawaii, solarpunk, *Serial Experiments Lain*-coded cyberpunk, and what one outlet called ["junk journaling for computers."](https://www.russh.com/the-return-of-the-cyberdeck/) What these aesthetics share is a rejection of the idea that computers have to look like gray boxes. If your deck lives inside a seashell clutch or a Polly Pocket compact, it says something about who you are and what you value — and that's the whole point.

## Cyberdeck Culture

While the term has cyberpunk roots, the cyberdeck community has grown far beyond any single aesthetic. You'll find solarpunk decks built for sustainability, cottagecore decks in wooden enclosures, hyper-femme decks in seashell purses, Lain-coded decks in thrifted electronics, and sleek minimalist builds that wouldn't look out of place in a design museum. What unites them all is the ethos: build it yourself, make it yours, share what you learn.

### Finding Your People

The cyberdeck community lives in a few places, and they have different vibes:

- **[Cyberdeck Cafe](https://cyberdeck.cafe)** — A welcoming, all-inclusive community with a Discord server, a build guide, and an explicit no-gatekeeping policy. If you're new, this is probably your best first stop.
- **[r/cyberDeck on Reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/cyberDeck/)** — The largest cyberdeck forum (~183,000 members), with a lot of build posts and inspiration. Worth browsing, though the community tone can be uneven.
- **TikTok** — Search [#cyberdeck](https://www.tiktok.com/discover/pretty-cyberdeck) or [#girly cyber deck](https://www.tiktok.com/discover/girly-cyber-deck). The comment sections under femme builds tend to be surprisingly supportive and full of beginner questions — a good place to lurk and learn.
- **cyberdeck.club** — You're here! We're building this wiki and community specifically for folks who want a space that centers all kinds of builders.

### The Broader Builder World

Cyberdecks connect to a wider maker ecosystem. Builders often overlap with communities around 3D printing, amateur radio, solarpunk and off-grid tech, creative coding, e-ink and e-reader projects, wearable electronics, and mesh networking. If you're drawn to one of these, you'll find kindred spirits in cyberdeck spaces — and vice versa.

On TikTok, cyberdeck content sits alongside a growing wave of femme maker content: creative coders like [@julip.mp3](https://www.tiktok.com/@julip.mp3/video/7451433459530190123) building custom vocoders, wearable-tech artists like [@createdbyeo](https://www.tiktok.com/@createdbyeo/video/7537363488243502366) making Sailor Moon headbands with embedded electronics, and the broader #womeninSTEM and #techgirlie communities. The boundaries between these worlds are porous and friendly.
